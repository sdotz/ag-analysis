2026 Club Challenge — age-graded scoring analysis across 4 races

Scoring rules & methodology
General rules (all models)
  • Team score = sum of the top 5 individual scores per race. If 4-5 runners score, at least 1 must be female. Teams of 1-3 have no gender requirement.
  • Season standing = best 8 race scores (lowest races dropped).
  • If the top 5 by score are all male, the lowest-scoring male is replaced by the highest-scoring female. If no female is available, the team falls back to 3 scorers.
1. Official AP% (current system)
  • Each runner's score is their age-graded percentage (AP%) from the 2025 WMA tables.
  • AP% = age-adjusted world record / actual time. A 75-year-old running 25:00 for 5K can outscore a 30-year-old running 19:00.
  • Bias: Prime-age world records are set by professional athletes (hard ceiling), while masters records are set by the best amateurs (easier ceiling). This makes it systematically easier for older runners to score high AP%, giving masters-heavy teams a structural advantage.
2. Decade Percentile (experimental)
  • Each runner is scored by percentile rank among runners in the same age decade (e.g., 40-49), gender, and race.
  • The fastest runner in a decade group scores 100%; the slowest scores near 0%.
  • Rationale: Normalizes for competitive depth differences across ages. A 70-year-old must beat other 70-somethings to score high, not just clear a world-record bar that few amateurs approach.
  • Limitation: Small decade groups (e.g., only 2 runners aged 70-79 in a race) produce coarse percentiles. Score depends on who else shows up in your decade, not absolute fitness.
3. Youth Requirement (experimental)
  • Teams must field at least 2 runners under age 35 among their top-5 scorers. An under-35 female can satisfy both the youth and female requirements.
  • If the team cannot field 2 under-35 runners, the youngest over-35 scorers filling those youth slots have their AP% recalculated at a baseline age of 35 — their actual finish time is scored against the age-35 standard instead of their real age standard.
  • Example: A 47-year-old with AP% 87.1% might drop to ~80% when scored at age 35. An 86-year-old with AP% 92.7% would drop to ~45.6%, but she would never be penalized because the penalty targets the youngest over-35 runners (closest to the cutoff), not the oldest.
  • Rationale: Incentivizes clubs to recruit and develop younger runners rather than relying entirely on masters depth. The penalty is proportional — runners just over 35 lose little, while the mechanism still allows masters runners to contribute at their full AP%.
